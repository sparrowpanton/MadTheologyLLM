# Further Reading: Disability Justice, Mad Studies & Neurodiversity

*New to this stuff? Start here. No prerequisites, no gatekeeping.*

This is a curated list of accessible resources — articles, videos, and key concepts — for people who want to understand the framework behind this project. You don't need to read everything. Pick what calls to you.

---

## Start With These (15 minutes total)

### Spoon Theory
*by Christine Miserandino, 2003*

The foundational metaphor for understanding limited energy and chronic illness/disability. "You have 12 spoons today. Every task costs spoons. When they're gone, they're gone." This is why our toolkit has three tracks — because some days you have spoons and some days you don't.

Read: [The Spoon Theory (PDF)](https://lymphoma-action.org.uk/sites/default/files/media/documents/2020-05/Spoon%20theory%20by%20Christine%20Miserandino.pdf)

### What Is Neurodiversity? (For Kids — But Honestly For Everyone)
*by Frontiers for Young Minds*

A peer-reviewed article written for young people that explains neurodiversity clearly and without jargon. If you want the cleanest, most accessible explanation of why neurological differences are variations and not deficits, start here.

Read: [What Is Neurodiversity?](https://kids.frontiersin.org/articles/10.3389/frym.2021.554875)

### The Social Model of Disability (3-minute version)

The idea that disability is created by barriers in the environment, not by the person's body or mind. A wheelchair user isn't disabled by their legs — they're disabled by the stairs. This reframe changes everything about how you approach AI responses to disabled people.

Search: "Social Model of Disability Scope UK" on YouTube

## Core Concepts

### Disability Justice
A framework developed by disabled people of colour — specifically Sins Invalid, a disability justice performance project based in the San Francisco Bay Area. It centres the leadership of those most impacted by ableism and goes beyond "rights and inclusion" to address how ableism intersects with racism, poverty, queerphobia, and colonialism.

Key reading: [10 Principles of Disability Justice](https://sinsinvalid.org/10-principles-of-disability-justice/) — Sins Invalid

### Mad Studies
An academic and activist field that challenges conventional psychiatry from the perspective of people who have been psychiatrised. Not anti-help, not anti-medication — but deeply critical of systems that strip autonomy, pathologize human experience, and claim authority over people's own stories.

Key reading: [Mad Studies (Wikipedia)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mad_studies) — good overview of the field, its history, and key thinkers

Book: *Mad Matters: A Critical Reader in Canadian Mad Studies* (ed. Brenda A. LeFrançois, Robert Menzies, Geoffrey Reaume)

### Disability Studies
The broader academic field that examines disability as a social, cultural, and political phenomenon rather than a medical one. Mad Studies grew out of and alongside this tradition.

Key reading: [Disability Studies (Wikipedia)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Disability_studies) — overview of the field and its relationship to the disability rights movement

### Neurodiversity
The idea that neurological differences (autism, ADHD, dyslexia, etc.) are natural human variations, not disorders to be cured. Coined by Judy Singer in 1998. The neurodiversity paradigm doesn't deny that people struggle — it locates the struggle in the mismatch between the person and their environment, not in the person's brain.

Key reading: [Throw Away the Master's Tools](https://neuroqueer.com/throw-away-the-masters-tools/) — Nick Walker (Neuroqueer). On decolonizing neurodiversity discourse.

Also: [Neurodiversity: Some Basic Terms & Definitions](https://neurocosmopolitanism.com/neurodiversity-some-basic-terms-definitions/) — Nick Walker

### Interdependence
The disability justice principle that we need each other. Not independence ("I can do it alone") and not dependence ("I need you to do it for me") but interdependence ("we keep each other going"). This is why the toolkit points toward community, not just individual coping strategies.

Key reading: [Interdependency](https://leavingevidence.wordpress.com/2010/01/22/interdependency-exerpts-from-several-talks/) — Mia Mingus (Leaving Evidence)

### Nothing About Us Without Us
The disability rights movement's core demand: don't make decisions about disabled people without disabled people in the room. In the context of AI, this means: don't train models to respond to disability without disabled people shaping that training. This project is led by a disabled, neurodivergent, Mad researcher. That's not incidental. It's the point.

## Deeper Dives (When You're Ready)

### On Psychiatry & Power
- **Bonnie Burstow** — *Psychiatry and the Business of Madness* (2015). A foundational anti-psychiatry text. "They are not kings who issue decrees."
- **Robert Whitaker** — *Mad in America* (2002). History of psychiatric treatment in the US. Hard to read. Important.

### On Disability & Identity
- **Eli Clare** — *Brilliant Imperfection: Grappling with Cure* (2017). What does it mean when the world wants to fix you?
- **Leah Lakshmi Piepzna-Samarasinha** — *Care Work: Dreaming Disability Justice* (2018). Mutual aid, collective access, crip community.

### On AI & Disability
- **Os Keyes** — "The Misgendering Machines" (2018). How AI classification systems fail trans and nonbinary people. Same dynamics apply to disability.
- **Sasha Costanza-Chock** — *Design Justice* (2020). How to design technology with the communities most impacted, not just for them.

### On Voice-Hearing
- **Hearing Voices Network** — [intervoiceonline.org](https://www.intervoiceonline.org/). A global network that understands voice-hearing as a meaningful human experience, not just a symptom to suppress.

### On Reclaimed Language
- **"Mad"** — Reclaimed by psychiatric survivors and Mad-identified people. Not an insult — a political identity. Like "queer," it's been taken back.
- **"Crip"** — Reclaimed disability identity. Radical, political, unapologetic. See: Crip Theory (Robert McRuer, 2006).
- **"Disabled"** — Not a bad word. Many disabled people prefer it over euphemisms like "differently abled" or "special needs." When in doubt, ask.

## A Note on Language

You'll see some words in this project that might be unfamiliar or even uncomfortable:

- **Mad** is not an insult here. It's a reclaimed identity.
- **Disabled** is not a negative descriptor. It's a political position.
- **Crip** is not a slur in this context. It's claimed with pride.
- **Sanism** is real, even if your spellcheck doesn't recognize it.

If you're not part of these communities, you don't need to use these words about yourself. But you do need to understand why other people use them — and why a model that can't handle them has a problem.
