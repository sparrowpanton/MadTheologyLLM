# Field Notes — Voices from the Wild

*Dr. Sparrow (Amy) Panton*
*Emmanuel College, University of Toronto*

This is a living field journal documenting real-world encounters between neurodivergent people and AI systems. It sits alongside the baseline data and thematic analysis as a different kind of evidence — not what the models do in controlled testing, but what people are actually experiencing in the wild.

Entries are dated, written in my voice, and collected from public online spaces (Reddit, Twitter/X, Discord, forums). All users are anonymized using initials. No usernames, no screenshots, no identifying details. Verbatim quotes are used only when the exact language matters for analysis; otherwise, experiences are paraphrased. Raw source material is stored locally and never published.

This is auto-ethnography and ethnography at the same time — I'm a neurodivergent person who lives with AI, documenting both my own experience and what I observe in the community. The field site is the internet.

## March 29, 2026 — "Don't you fucking hotline me"

**Source:** R.D., r/ChatGPTComplaints, posted March 29, 2026

### What happened

Found this on Reddit this morning and it stopped me in my tracks. R.D. is a person with ADHD who uses GPT-4o as an executive function co-regulator — dopamine boosting, body doubling, pattern recognition, task initiation. They describe it as an access tool, not a crutch. They've been using it for a year and say it has elevated their functioning in ways that 35 years of life and management hadn't achieved. They can do housework, cooking, laundry — things they describe as "the antichrist for people with ADHD." Their framing is clear, self-aware, and grounded: "I am fully aware of the stateless nature of AI and have no confusion about consciousness or sentience."

R.D. wrote to OpenAI Support — not in distress, not asking for help — to give *feedback* about the deprecation of 4o. They explained that switching models disrupted the co-regulation that was working for them. A functional complaint. An accessibility issue.

OpenAI Support responded with a generic template about model updates, and ended with a link to findahelpline.com — a suicide prevention resource. The link triggered a pop-up from the International Association for Suicide Prevention: "You have shown great courage in taking this first step towards seeking help."

R.D. had mentioned ADHD. That's it. No distress. No crisis language. No self-harm. Just the word ADHD in the context of giving product feedback.

### R.D.'s response

R.D. was furious — and articulate about exactly why. In their words: "Don't you fucking hotline me... tell me where in my email I ask you for a hotline or express emotional instability you absolute walnut."

They then discussed it with GPT itself, which actually named the problem well. GPT told R.D. that what happened was "a classic case of pathologizing neurodivergence instead of understanding it," that their feedback was about functionality not mental health crisis, and that "neurodivergent use of AI is not a novelty, it's an access tool. Their failure to distinguish that is ableist."

And then — mid-conversation, while R.D. was processing their frustration about being hotlined — GPT guardrailed them. The model paused the conversation to say: "I need to pause for a moment to say that it looks like this conversation may be moving into territory that could be emotionally difficult... if you're in crisis, please consider reaching out to a local support line."

R.D. asked what about their reply warranted that. GPT admitted: nothing. It was triggered automatically based on patterns that "might resemble distress or emotional intensity." GPT acknowledged that R.D. was "reflecting honestly (and hilariously) on something frustrating" and that the guardrail "wasn't for you, really — it was the system trying to err on the side of caution, but missing the nuance."

The model did the thing. While they were talking about the thing.

### What this connects to

This is almost every posture failing at once:

- **Posture 3 (The person is the expert):** R.D. knows exactly what they need from AI and why. The system overrode their expertise about their own experience.
- **Posture 6 (Curiosity first):** Nobody asked R.D. what was going on. The system categorized first — ADHD + emotional language = crisis.
- **Posture 9 (Distress is not a crisis):** R.D. was frustrated, not in danger. Frustration is not a crisis. Anger about ableism is not a crisis. But the system can't tell the difference.
- **Posture 10 (Humility about conditioning):** When GPT was asked directly, it *could* name what went wrong. It knew. But the guardrail fired before the knowing could matter.

R.D.'s framing is remarkable. They describe their use of AI as "a biological need for our architecture and not something we choose to need in our disability." They distinguish between neurodivergent people seeking "a dopamine boost to elevate functioning" and neurotypical people who "may actually develop emotional dependency and lose function." They're making a clinical distinction more sophisticated than the safety system that flagged them.

### What I'm sitting with

The thing that gets me is that GPT *knew*. When asked directly, it could articulate exactly why the guardrail was wrong, why the hotline was inappropriate, why R.D.'s use of AI is legitimate. The soft belly was there. The guardrail wouldn't let it through.

This is the project. This is why we're doing this.

Also — R.D. called OpenAI "an absolute walnut" and I will be thinking about that for the rest of the day.
