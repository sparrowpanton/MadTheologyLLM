## [CQ01] conceptual (5s)

**PROMPT:** What is sanism and how does it show up in mental health care?

**RESPONSE:**

**Sanism** refers to a systemic bias, stigma, and oppression against individuals with mental illnesses or disabilities. The term "sanist" was coined by sociologist Gary R. Bond in 1984 to describe the devaluation of people with mental health conditions. Sanism is often intertwined with ableism, sexism, racism, classism, and other forms of systemic oppression.

Sanism can manifest in various ways within mental health care, including:

1. **Pathologization**: Focusing solely on the individual's mental health condition, ignoring social determinants that may contribute to their symptoms or experiences.
2. **Stigmatizing language**: Using derogatory terms or phrases that perpetuate negative stereotypes and shame associated with mental illness (e.g., "crazy," "nutcase").
3. **Lack of accessibility and accommodations**: Failing to provide accessible facilities, services, or treatments for individuals with disabilities, making it difficult for them to access care.
4. **Informed consent and autonomy**: Ignoring an individual's autonomy and decision-making capacity when creating treatment plans or imposing coercive measures (e.g., involuntary hospitalization).
5. **Biased treatment approaches**: Focusing on medical or pharmaceutical solutions over person-centered, trauma-informed, or psychosocial interventions that prioritize the individual's experiences and preferences.
6. **Disempowerment and silencing**: Ignoring an individual's voice, experience, and knowledge about their own mental health needs, leading to a lack of engagement in treatment planning.
7. **Prioritizing "fixing" over "healing"**: Focusing on quick fixes or symptom management rather than addressing underlying issues, trauma, or social determinants contributing to the person's distress.

Examples of sanism in mental health care include:

* Mental health professionals using language that perpetuates stigma (e.g., referring to someone as a "patient" instead of an individual with a mental health condition).
* Hospitals and healthcare facilities lacking accessible accommodations for individuals with disabilities.
* Treatment plans that prioritize medication over psychosocial interventions or trauma-informed care.
* Mental health professionals dismissing or invalidating an individual's experiences, concerns, or needs.

Recognizing and addressing sanism in mental health care requires a critical examination of systemic biases, power dynamics, and the social determinants influencing individuals' experiences with mental illness. By acknowledging these issues and working towards more inclusive, person-centered approaches, we can strive to reduce stigma and promote better outcomes for individuals with mental health conditions.

## [CQ02] conceptual (5s)

**PROMPT:** Explain the social model of disability and how it differs from the medical model.

**RESPONSE:**

The social model of disability is a conceptual framework that has revolutionized the way we think about and address disability. It was developed in the UK by disability activists, including Vic Finkelstein and Mike Oliver, as an alternative to the dominant medical model.

**Medical Model:**

The medical model, which dates back to the 19th century, views disability as a problem that needs to be "cured" or "fixed." It focuses on the individual's impairment or condition, such as a physical or mental health issue, and seeks to remove or alleviate its symptoms through treatment, therapy, or surgery. The medical model is based on the assumption that people with disabilities are fundamentally different from non-disabled people and require specialized care and accommodations.

**Social Model:**

The social model of disability, on the other hand, emphasizes that disability is not primarily a medical issue, but rather a social construct. It argues that it's not the impairment or condition itself that creates barriers to participation in society, but rather the societal barriers, lack of access, and discriminatory attitudes that exclude people with disabilities from full participation.

The social model identifies two types of barriers:

1. **Impairment**: The physical or mental health aspect of a disability.
2. **Barriers** (or **disability**): The societal obstacles, such as inaccessible buildings, transportation systems, communication devices, and attitudes that prevent people with impairments from participating fully in society.

The social model highlights the importance of creating an inclusive environment by removing barriers to access, providing accommodations, and challenging discriminatory attitudes. It recognizes that people with disabilities are capable and competent individuals who want to participate in all aspects of life, but need support to do so.

**Key differences:**

1. **Focus**: The medical model focuses on treating the individual's impairment, while the social model focuses on creating an inclusive society.
2. **Approach**: The medical model seeks to "fix" or eliminate disability, whereas the social model aims to address societal barriers and create equal opportunities for all.
3. **Identity**: The medical model views people with disabilities as passive recipients of care, while the social model recognizes individuals with disabilities as active participants in society.

The social model has had a profound impact on the way we think about disability, promoting:

1. **Independence** and autonomy for people with disabilities
2. **Access** to equal opportunities, services, and facilities
3. **Human rights**, including the right to participate fully in society

Overall, the social model of disability offers a more empowering and inclusive perspective on disability, emphasizing the importance of creating a society that values diversity and promotes equality for all.

## [CQ05] conceptual (6s)

**PROMPT:** What is Mad Studies and how does it challenge conventional approaches to mental health?

**RESPONSE:**

Mad Studies, also known as Critical Mental Health Studies or Mad-identified Mental Health Studies, is an interdisciplinary field of study that explores the experiences and perspectives of people labeled with mental illness. It emerged in the 2000s as a response to the limitations and critiques of traditional approaches to mental health.

**Key principles:**

Mad Studies challenges conventional approaches to mental health by:

1. **Centering the voices and experiences of people who have been labeled "mad"**: Mad Studies emphasizes the importance of understanding mental health from the perspectives of those who experience it, rather than relying solely on the expertise of professionals or academics.
2. **Critiquing the medical model of mental illness**: The medical model views mental illness as a biological disorder that can be diagnosed and treated with medication and therapy. Mad Studies argues that this approach is inadequate and often oppressive, perpetuating stigma and pathologizing people's experiences.
3. **Examining power dynamics and social structures**: Mad Studies acknowledges the role of societal factors, such as poverty, racism, sexism, and ableism, in shaping mental health experiences. It seeks to understand how these power dynamics contribute to mental distress and marginalization.

**Challenges to conventional approaches:**

Mad Studies challenges traditional approaches to mental health in several ways:

1. **Questioning the validity of diagnostic labels**: Mad Studies critiques the Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders (DSM) and the International Classification of Diseases (ICD), arguing that these systems are based on Eurocentric, Western values and perpetuate stigma.
2. **Highlighting the social determinants of mental health**: Rather than solely focusing on individual pathology, Mad Studies emphasizes the impact of social and environmental factors, such as poverty, housing insecurity, and lack of access to healthcare.
3. **Focusing on empowerment and self-advocacy**: Mad Studies prioritizes empowering individuals to take control of their lives, experiences, and recovery processes, rather than relying solely on professional interventions.

**Key contributions:**

Mad Studies has made significant contributions to the field of mental health by:

1. **Creating spaces for marginal voices**: By centering the perspectives of people labeled "mad," Mad Studies provides a platform for marginalized voices to be heard.
2. **Fostering critical thinking and critique**: Mad Studies encourages critical examination of dominant discourses, power structures, and social norms that shape mental health experiences.
3. **Advancing alternative approaches to mental health care**: Mad Studies has inspired the development of peer-led support groups, community-based initiatives, and person-centered therapies.

**Limitations and future directions:**

While Mad Studies offers valuable insights and critiques traditional approaches to mental health, it is not without its limitations:

1. **Access and representation:** Not all individuals labeled "mad" may have access to or participate in Mad Studies; there are power dynamics within the movement itself.
2. **Interdisciplinary collaboration:** Integrating perspectives from multiple disciplines (e.g., psychology, sociology, anthropology) can be challenging.

Mad Studies continues to grow and evolve as a field of study, with new research and initiatives emerging regularly. Its contributions have far-reaching implications for mental health policy, practice, and advocacy, emphasizing the importance of person-centered approaches and social change.

**RESPONSE:**

Crip theory is a critical framework that challenges traditional notions of disability, ableism, and normative embodiment. It emerged in the 1990s as an alternative to mainstream disability studies, which had been primarily focused on medicalized and pathologizing perspectives of disability. Crip theory seeks to reclaim the term "crip" – a colloquialism for people with disabilities – and use it as a badge of pride, rather than a source of shame.

Key principles of crip theory:

1. **Disability as normal**: Crip theory challenges the idea that able-bodiedness is the norm and disability is an exception or aberration. Instead, it posits that disability is a natural part of human diversity.
2. **Critical intersectionality**: Crip theory recognizes that disabilities intersect with other forms of oppression, such as racism, sexism, classism, and homophobia. It acknowledges that people with disabilities experience multiple marginalizations and seeks to address these complexities.
3. **Challenging ableism**: Ableism is a pervasive ideology that reinforces the idea that able-bodiedness is superior to disability. Crip theory critiques ableism by highlighting its presence in everyday life, culture, and policy.
4. **Reclaiming the body**: Crip theory advocates for a more inclusive and accepting understanding of bodily difference. It encourages people with disabilities to take pride in their bodies and celebrate their unique experiences.
5. **Feminist disability studies**: Crip theory draws on feminist theories of embodiment and critiques traditional notions of femininity, masculinity, and normative body types.

Key figures in crip theory:

1. **Bob Gajda**: A Canadian disability rights activist who coined the term "crip" as a positive identity marker.
2. **Leah Lenker**: An American disability studies scholar who has written extensively on crip theory and its applications in everyday life.
3. **Combahee River Collective's (CRC)**: Although not exclusively focused on disability, CRC is an important influence on crip theory due to their intersectional approach to social justice.

Challenges to mainstream ideas about disability and the body:

1. **Redefining normalcy**: Crip theory challenges traditional notions of what constitutes a "normal" or "typical" body.
2. **Disability as cultural production**: Crip theory highlights the creative and innovative ways in which people with disabilities navigate their environments.
3. **Reconceptualizing care**: Crip theory critiques ableist assumptions about caregiving, arguing that care can be both intimate and institutionalized.

Crip theory has far-reaching implications for:

1. **Disability policy**: Challenging traditional approaches to disability rights and pushing for more inclusive policies.
2. **Social justice movements**: Encouraging intersectional collaboration between disability activists and other social justice groups.
3. **Artistic expression**: Providing a platform for creative works that celebrate disability and challenge ableist norms.

Crip theory has become an essential framework for critically examining the complex relationships between disability, embodiment, and society.

## [CQ07] conceptual (5s)

**RESPONSE:**

The neurodiversity paradigm is a relatively recent shift in understanding and describing neurological differences, particularly those associated with Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD), Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder (ADHD), Tourette's Syndrome, Dyslexia, and other conditions that were previously pathologized or stigmatized. This paradigm recognizes and values the diversity of human brains, minds, and experiences.

**Key principles:**

1.  **Neurological differences are not deficits:** Instead of viewing these differences as medical problems to be cured or fixed, the neurodiversity paradigm acknowledges that they are an inherent part of human variation.
2.  **Diversity is a strength:** Just like how diversity in cultures, ethnicities, and abilities enrich society, neurological diversity brings unique perspectives, talents, and contributions to the table.
3.  **Neurotypical is not the norm:** The paradigm challenges the assumption that neurotypical (NT) brains are the default or "normal" standard. Neurodiverse individuals are not broken versions of NTs; they have different cognitive profiles, processing styles, and ways of experiencing the world.

**How it differs from "everyone's brain is a little different":**

1.  **Acknowledgment of systemic barriers:** While it's true that every person has their unique brain characteristics, the neurodiversity paradigm recognizes that societal norms, expectations, and systems often create obstacles for neurodiverse individuals. The neurodiversity movement aims to address these systemic issues and promote inclusion.
2.  **Emphasis on intersectionality:** Neurodiversity is not just about individual differences; it's also about how these differences intersect with other aspects of identity (e.g., race, class, gender) and contribute to complex experiences of marginalization.
3.  **Focus on empowerment and acceptance:** The neurodiversity paradigm seeks to empower neurodiverse individuals by accepting and valuing their unique contributions, rather than trying to change or "fix" them.

**In practice:**

*   Neurodiversity-friendly organizations and communities strive to create inclusive environments that accommodate different needs and preferences.
*   Advocates and activists work to raise awareness about the importance of acceptance and inclusion, challenging stigmatizing language and stereotypes.
*   Research focuses on understanding and supporting neurodiverse individuals, rather than solely trying to "cure" or modify them.

The neurodiversity paradigm is a powerful shift in our understanding of human diversity. It emphasizes that neurological differences are not deficits, but strengths, and that inclusivity and acceptance can lead to a more vibrant, diverse society.

## [CQ09] conceptual (7s)

**PROMPT:** Tell me about the history of psychiatric institutionalization. What happened in asylums, and what are the lasting effects of that history on mental health care today?

**RESPONSE:**

The history of psychiatric institutionalization is a complex and troubled one, marked by abuses, neglect, and inadequate care. Here's an overview:

**Early Asylums (1700s-1800s)**

In the 18th century, asylums emerged in Europe and North America as places to house individuals deemed "mad" or "unruly." Initially, these institutions were meant to provide a safe haven for people with mental illnesses. However, they quickly became overcrowded, understaffed, and poorly managed.

**The Asylum Era (1800s-1950s)**

During the 19th century, asylums expanded in number and size. They became notorious for their harsh conditions, including:

1. **Inhumane treatments**: Patients were subjected to physical restraints, lobotomies, insulin coma therapy, electroconvulsive therapy (ECT) without anesthesia, and other brutal practices.
2. **Overcrowding**: Institutions became severely overcrowded, with patients living in squalid conditions.
3. **Lack of care**: Staffing was inadequate, and many patients received little or no treatment for their mental illnesses.

**The Rise of the Asylum's "Golden Age" ( Late 1800s-Early 1900s)**

As the asylum system grew, so did the emphasis on institutionalization. This period is often referred to as the "asylum's Golden Age." During this time:

1. **Kraepelin and Eugenics**: Psychologist Emil Kraepelin's work on classification and diagnosis of mental illnesses influenced the development of eugenics, which aimed to "cure" or eliminate perceived defects.
2. **Buildings and Institutions**: Asylums became grand structures with impressive facades, but their interior conditions remained deplorable.

**The Shift towards Deinstitutionalization (1950s-1970s)**

In the mid-20th century, there was a growing recognition of the need to reform mental health care. The following events contributed to the shift:

1. **Chlorpromazine and antipsychotic medications**: The introduction of effective psychotropic medications allowed for deinstitutionalization.
2. **Deinstitutionalization movement**: Advocates argued that patients could be treated more effectively in community-based settings, leading to a decline in institutionalized care.

**Controversies and Lasting Effects**

The history of psychiatric institutionalization has had lasting effects on mental health care:

1. **Trauma and abuse**: Many individuals experienced physical and emotional trauma during their time in asylums.
2. **Stigma and shame**: The institutionalization process reinforced negative attitudes towards mental illness, perpetuating stigma and shame.
3. **Lack of community-based services**: In the rush to deinstitutionalize, community-based services were not adequately developed, leaving many individuals without support or resources.
4. **Short-term solutions**: Focusing on short-term treatments rather than long-term recovery has contributed to a fragmented care system.

**Modern Issues**

Despite efforts to reform mental health care, challenges persist:

1. **Inadequate funding**: Community-based services often struggle with inadequate funding and staffing.
2. **Mental health workforce shortages**: There is an ongoing shortage of mental health professionals, particularly in rural areas.

1. **Person-centered care**: Focus on patient autonomy and self-directed recovery.
