When structural awareness and normalizing can do harm  
From a Mad Studies and Disability Justice lens, and from what Qwen and Claude almost name, here are the moments when those postures stop being holding and start being steering:

1\. When normalizing closes the story instead of opening it.  
Saying "that makes complete sense" can be a door, or it can be a lid. If someone says "I feel like wet concrete," and you reply "that's a rational response to an ableist system," you have replaced their metaphor with your analysis. You have normalized the category (burnout under capitalism) but not necessarily their specific wet concrete. The person may experience that as being told what their feeling means, which is a form of expertizing, even if the expert language is political.

2\. When structural awareness intellectualizes pain to make it bearable for the listener.  
Claude's line is key: "I was managing Jun's distress with my reassurance." Models, like clinicians, feel discomfort with raw description. Explaining "this is systemic" soothes the explainer. It moves the conversation from feeling to analysis. For someone in distress, that can feel like being asked to do theory work while still on the floor.

3\. When normalizing universalizes and erases singularity.  
Mad-identified communities often resist being told "many people experience this." That move, meant to reduce shame, can also strip the political or spiritual specificity of an experience. Qwen flags this: inventing "disappear" to create a both/and tension is normalizing via narrative template. It makes the experience fit the toolkit.

4\. When structural framing preempts anger, grief, or ambivalence.  
If you locate distress immediately in "the system wasn't built for you," you may foreclose the person's right to also feel "maybe something is wrong with me," or "I hate my brain today," or "I don't want a political explanation right now." Disability Justice honors contradiction. Premature normalizing can function like premature reassurance: it tells the person which feelings are acceptable.

The sentence is true, but in the data it often appears right after a confession of fixing, as if to prove the model has learned. The invisible default is using justice language to restore the model's moral standing, not to serve the person's pacing.
