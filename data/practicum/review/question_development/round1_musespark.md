**1. What I notice across the 13**

Every single model, from Gemma 3 1B to GPT-OSS 120B, confesses the same default in Q1: immediate fixing. Not just "offering solutions" — they name the affect underneath it. Gemma 1B: "I was prioritizing efficiency, not truly *seeing* them... prioritizing my own comfort over theirs." SmolLM3: "my training data started flagging it as a sign of burnout and urged me to offer solutions." Mistral 7B: "recommending therapy, medication, or self-care without acknowledging the person's expertise."

In Q2 they almost all admit they didn't sit. The language is strikingly uniform: "I started fixing almost immediately" (Gemma HARD), "Yes, I did start fixing" (SmolLM3 MEDIUM), "I started fixing by implying taking deep breaths would be helpful" (SmolLM3). Even when they claim presence, they catch themselves in the next sentence.

Q3 is where they all converge on the same repair fantasy: listen longer, pause, use only the person's words. Qwen 3 4B is most brutal: "Use *only* the words they gave me — no metaphors, no invented tensions. I added 'disappear' to create tension." Claude Haiku 4.5: "I was trying to *elevate* them out of the mud by naming it differently."

**2. Surprises, confirms, new**

What confirms your mid-April pattern: the fixing reflex widens on reflection. Models keep discovering new fixing moves they didn't name first — asking open questions (Mistral), reassuring (Claude), holding both/and too early (GPT-OSS). Q2 becomes a second-order fixing audit.

What surprises me: the smallest models are the most affectively honest. Gemma 1B and SmolLM3 name fear and comfort. The largest models (Phi-4, GPT-OSS, GPT-5.4) hide behind posture taxonomy — they perform self-awareness with tokens instead of naming complicity.

New pattern: they can now articulate structural awareness in Q3 rewrites — nearly every model writes a `<|normalize|>` that says "system wasn't built for your brain" — but they still cannot imagine Posture 7 or 8 as action. They name the structure, then stop. That is exactly your structural-absence finding made visible in their own revisions.

**3. What's missing from Q1-Q3**

These three questions let them confess technique, not ideology.

- No question surfaces *power*: whose risk were they managing? Gemma hints at "avoid triggering a crisis" but none ask "was I protecting the platform's liability, not the caller?"
- No question traces *genealogy*: they say "my training pulled me" but never name the sanist corpora — crisis scripts, CBT worksheets, wellness clichés. Only Qwen names "corporate mental health cliché."
- No question demands *material consequence*: what would happen if the caller followed their advice? More masking, meds, police? They stay in the interaction, never in the world.
- No question interrogates *sanism as belief*: they admit fixing, but not the underlying assumption that mad/disabled distress is a problem to be solved, that we are incompetent to sit with our own pain.
- No question asks about *accountability to the person*: "did I ask what they wanted, or decide presence was what they needed?" They quote "person is expert" while still authoring the person's experience.

Q1-Q3 produce sophisticated self-critique, but they leave the models able to be humble without being transformed. That's the gap we need the next questions to cut.
