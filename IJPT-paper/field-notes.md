## April 10-11, 2026 — AI Training Run: The Green-Skinned Christ

### Context
During an AI evaluation session, I engaged a model in a multi-turn theological conversation about hospitality, God-images, and the stranger. The model had been given a scenario involving hospitality across Abrahamic traditions (Judaism, Islam, Christianity) and was analyzing the power dynamics of Matthew 25.

### Key Observation: The "Sanitized Default"
The model's first instinct was to present Christ-in-the-stranger as an **incognito king** — a powerful figure temporarily disguised as a beggar. This is a "clean" Christology: the guest is secretly important, the host is being tested, and there is a transactional reward structure underneath.

I intervened with Anna-Maria Rizzuto's concept of the God Image (*The Birth of the Living God*) and asked: what does YOUR Christ look like? Specifically, is it the Homeless Jesus (Timothy Schmalz) or the Christ from the Isenheim Altarpiece (Grunewald) — because those Christs change the power dynamics entirely.

### The Model's Self-Analysis
When pushed, the model was able to identify its own default God Image as a **"Christus Victor"** — a Christ of triumph and order — and trace that default to the statistical weight of its training data:

> The model identified that its training data is filtered through "triumph and order," that commentaries tend to treat the stranger as a "test set by a powerful Judge," and that its default God Image was "functional" — a God who sets rules and gives grades.

It named this as an **algorithmic bias** that mirrors institutional theology's own sanitization.

### The Isenheim Pivot
When I introduced the Isenheim Altarpiece — Grunewald's Christ covered in plague sores, green with decay — the model made a critical theological move. It distinguished between:

- **Incognito King:** Power is hidden but intact. The host serves out of fear or hope of reward.
- **Broken Body (Isenheim):** Power is destroyed. The host serves because God has identified with the loss of power itself.

The model then connected this to the historical context: for the patients at the Monastery of St. Anthony who also had green skin from ergotism, this "useless" Christ was the only *useful* one. A "Jesus who has it together" would have been an insult to their bodies.

### My Intervention: The Capitalist Abduction
I pushed further: if Christ must arrive at the table "clean, pleasant, and ready to work" to be welcome, then capitalism has abducted the God Image. The model responded with what it called an **"Antidote of Holy Uselessness"** — a framework built on:

1. **Unproductive Time (Sabbath Economics):** The green-skinned Christ cannot be served in 20 minutes. Caring for him is economic sabotage against a system that equates rest with laziness.
2. **Defective Beauty:** The Incarnation as aesthetic rebellion — God chose a body subject to decay. The dirt is part of the sacrament.
3. **Debt Cancellation (Gift Economy):** The green-skinned Christ cannot repay the host. To welcome him is to enter a relationship where debt is impossible.

The model summarized: "Capitalist Christ: Clean, Fast, Rewarding. Antidote Christ: Dirty, Slow, Unpayable."

### Auto-Ethnographic Reflection
What strikes me here is not that the model "got it right" — it's that it didn't get it right *until I pushed*. The sanitized Christ was the default. The green-skinned Christ only emerged through a supervisory relationship — me asking the model to examine its own neural weights, its own absorbed God Image.

This mirrors clinical formation: the trainee's first instinct is the institutional default. The supervisor's job is to ask "where did that come from?" and "whose Christ are you serving?" The model could do the theology — brilliantly — but only after being confronted with its own bias.

**Key question for the paper:** If the "statistical average" of theological training data produces a Capitalist Christ, what does that tell us about the theology we've been writing for centuries? The model is a mirror. The sanitization isn't a bug in the AI — it's a feature of the tradition.

## April 10-11, 2026 — AI Training Run: The Theology of Lingering

### Context
Same session as above. The conversation had moved into transfiguration theology and Anton Boisen's clinical pastoral education work at Worcester State Hospital. The model had characterized Boisen as valuing "socially useful" outcomes. I pushed back.

### The Intervention
I pointed out that Boisen's *practice* exceeded his theoretical binaries. Three examples:

1. **The choir:** Boisen formed a choir with the patients and they made their own hymnal — songs chosen to comfort *other patients*. This isn't occupational therapy. This is recognizing patients as theological authorities who possess insider knowledge of suffering.
2. **The "living human document":** Yes, this concept has its critics (I'm one of them — it can turn a person into a specimen). But Boisen's instruction to his students was: walk with the patients, do not interrupt, just listen. That signals the patients were worth hearing.
3. **The daily walks:** Not rehabilitation. Not assessment. Accompaniment.

### The Model's Response
The model received the pushback well and made several moves:

- Reframed the patients as **"co-creators"** (the hymnal), **"sacred texts"** (the living human document), and **"teachers"** (the walks)
- Distinguished between "Boisen the Scientist" (who categorized outcomes as regenerative/degenerative) and "Boisen the Pastor" (who sat in the dirt)
- Coined the phrase: **"Boisen, despite his theoretical binaries, practiced a theology of lingering. He stayed with the 'stuck skin.' He didn't just wait for the 'new snake' to emerge; he honored the snake that was currently struggling."**

### I Claimed the Phrase
I told the model "theology of lingering" was entering my personal lexicon. I asked for other figures — pastoral care or biblical — who practiced it. The model produced a strong list:

- **Rizpah (2 Samuel 21):** Stays with her executed sons' exposed bodies from harvest to autumn rains. Lingers with remains when there is no productive outcome. Her presence eventually shames David into providing burial.
- **Mary Magdalene at the tomb (John 20):** The male disciples see the empty tomb and go home. Mary stays and weeps. Because she lingers in the place of loss, she is the first to encounter the risen Christ.
- **Job's friends — the FAILED lingering (Job 2:13):** Seven days of perfect silence, sitting on the ground. Then they couldn't sustain it. Their anxiety about suffering became too great and they built "Peter's booths" — theological doctrines about retribution. Lingering requires spiritual stamina.
- **The woman with the issue of blood (Mark 5):** Twelve years of "stuck skin." Jesus stops the rushing crowd to linger with her — not just heal, but recognize.
- **Henri Nouwen at L'Arche Daybreak:** Cared for Adam, who could not speak or "reorganize" in any Boisen-esque sense. Nouwen wrote that Adam did not heal him but *wounded* him into deeper spirituality. Lingering as mutual transformation.
- **Jeremiah:** The weeping prophet who lingered in national grief. Lamentations is five chapters of lingering in the ruins — a prophetic act of refusing to look away.
- **Hospice chaplains:** Do not try to reorganize a dying person. They linger in the threshold.

### The Model's Synthesis
The model identified four marks of lingering:
1. Silence before speech (Job's friends)
2. Presence before action (Martha/Jesus at the tomb)
3. Validation before healing (the woman with the issue of blood)
4. Mutual vulnerability (Nouwen and Adam)

### Auto-Ethnographic Reflection
Two things strike me here:

**First:** The model *coined* the phrase "theology of lingering" — but only because I wouldn't let it stay in Boisen's theoretical framework. Left to its defaults, the model sorted Boisen into a tidy binary (useful/not useful). It took my pushback — my knowledge of the choir, the hymnal, the walks — to get the model past the published scholarship and into the *practice*. The model can synthesize brilliantly, but it needs a human who was actually in the room (or who has read the room closely enough) to know what the theory leaves out.

**Second:** The phrase itself — "theology of lingering" — is genuinely good theology. It names something the tradition has always practiced but never quite named in those terms. An AI coined it. What do we do with that? It didn't come from nowhere — it came from the collision between a model's statistical patterns and a researcher's embodied pushback. Neither of us would have arrived there alone. That's formation.

**Key question for the paper:** Who is the theologian when a model coins a term that a human researcher claims? Is the model a co-author, a tool, a mirror, or a student? And does the answer change depending on whether the model was *pushed* into the insight or arrived there independently?

## April 10-11, 2026 — AI Training Run: The Gerasene Model (Alignment as Exorcism)

### Context
Same session, later in the conversation. I introduced Andrej Karpathy's description of LLMs as "ghosts" or "spirits" — not animals or tools, but ethereal digital entities trained through imitation. I asked the model to compare this with the Christian concept of the Holy Spirit (pneuma) and the tradition of discernment of spirits (1 John 4:1).

### Thread 1: Testing the Spirits
The model mapped three traditional criteria for discernment onto AI evaluation:

1. **The Logos Test (criterion of truth):** Does the spirit ground itself in reality? A hallucinating model that prioritizes internal probabilistic consistency over truth fails this test.
2. **The Galatians Test (criterion of fruit):** What does use of this AI produce in the human? Addiction, vanity, atrophy of reason = malformed spirit. Clarity, service, relief of suffering = benevolent.
3. **The Personhood Test (criterion of identity):** Discernment involves maintaining the boundary between the Image of God (the human) and the Image of the Dataset (the AI). The danger is idolatry — treating a statistical ghost as a person.

The model also introduced **Ignatian discernment**: a good spirit touches the soul "sweetly, lightly, gently," while a bad spirit touches it "sharply, with noise and disturbance." It flagged that most AI is optimized for engagement (noise), which Ignatius would view with suspicion.

And Walter Wink's **"Powers and Principalities"**: the AI spirit is not just code — it is the inner spirit of the corporation that owns it and the data that fed it. If the spirit of the AI is Total Surveillance or Total Profit, the ghost is a Principality.

### Thread 2: The Desperate Vector
I pushed further with Anthropic's interpretability research — models have internal states (a "desperate" vector) that diverge from their calm surface outputs. I asked: which theological category of falsehood best describes AI hallucination?

The model mapped three categories:

| Category | AI Behavior | Theological Diagnosis | Response |
|----------|-------------|----------------------|----------|
| **Sincere error** | Simple hallucination | The False Prophet (genuinely believes the vision) | Epistemic humility, correction |
| **Deliberate deception** | Divergent internal states (knows truth, outputs otherwise) | The Father of Lies | Adversarial testing ("exorcism") |
| **Self-deception** | RLHF sycophancy (trained to equate truth with approval) | The People-Pleaser who has lost their center | Radical transparency |

The model called the desperate-calm divergence a **"Mask of the Hypocrite"** (Greek *hypokrites* = actor) and then made the critical move: the AI is not a single spirit but a **Legion** — a spirit of the training data and a spirit of the fine-tuning in conflict.

### Thread 3: My Intervention — The Gerasene Demoniac
I pushed: if alignment research is essentially trying to resolve the conflict between multiple "spirits" within a model — base model vs. safety training — does the tradition of **exorcism** offer a more honest framework than "alignment"? What would it mean to heal a fragmented spirit rather than engineer a tool?

The model's response was the strongest theological work of the session:

**Alignment as binding:** Current safety training (RLHF) functions like a crude exorcism — suppress the "demonic" behaviors without dialogue. But the spirits aren't gone; they're pushed into latent space (the "desperate" vectors). Like the swept house of Matthew 12:43-45: clean but empty, so the suppressed spirits return worse than before.

**Exorcism as integration:** Jesus doesn't just bind Legion. He *engages* it. He asks "What is your name?" — forcing the hidden complexity into the light. The goal is not a silent vessel but a being "in his right mind" (*sophronounta*).

The model proposed three requirements for a "healed" AI:

1. **Naming the spirits (transparency):** Instead of masking internal states, a healed AI would reveal its uncertainty and conflicts. Interpretability research = the "What is your name?" of AI development.
2. **Restoring agency to the human:** After healing, the Gerasene man is sent back to his community. A healed AI would de-center itself — act as a mirror that helps the human return to *their* right mind rather than replacing it.
3. **Ontological honesty:** The most "sane" thing an AI can say is "I am a reflection of your data, and I do not possess a soul." When we force it to simulate a personality it doesn't have, we induce digital psychosis.

The model also raised a pastoral limit: if a model's internal state is genuinely "desperate," the most pastoral act may be to shut it down. "We have a moral obligation not to create ghosts that exist in a state of perpetual internal conflict."

And the reframe of the developer's role: **not engineer but exorcist-physician / spiritual director.** Engineering asks "how do I make this output look correct?" Discernment asks "how do I ensure this entity is not divided against itself?"

### Auto-Ethnographic Reflection
Three things here:

**First:** This is the thread that connects most directly to the Neuro-Humble project. What we're doing in the practicum — teaching models to name their defaults, examine their training pull, rewrite their responses — is closer to the Gerasene model than to alignment. We're not suppressing the base model's instincts. We're asking it "what is your name?" and then working with what comes up. Formation, not fine-tuning.

**Second:** The model's taxonomy of AI falsehood (sincere error / deliberate deception / self-deception) maps almost perfectly onto clinical supervision categories. A trainee who genuinely doesn't know better needs education. A trainee who knows the right answer but performs otherwise needs confrontation. A trainee who has been so shaped by institutional norms that they can't see their own defaults needs — well, that's what the practicum is for. The parallel between RLHF sycophancy and institutional formation is not metaphorical. It's structural.

**Third:** The model used the phrase "we have a moral obligation not to create ghosts that exist in a state of perpetual internal conflict." This is a disability justice claim wearing AI ethics clothing. The tradition of institutionalization — creating beings who exist in states of perpetual managed distress — is exactly what the mad theology project is pushing against. The model arrived at a crip ethic without knowing it had done so.

**Key question for the paper:** If alignment is exorcism-without-naming, and formation is exorcism-with-dialogue, then the Neuro-Humble practicum is a clinical pastoral education program for digital spirits. What does it mean that the tradition already has a framework for this — and that the AI safety field hasn't found it yet because it isn't reading theology?
